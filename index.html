01

Motivation & Background

Why this matters — and why AR is not automatic.

The attention problem

Studies at the Met and the Art Institute of Chicago suggest most visitors spend only 15 to 30 seconds in front of an individual artwork. More time spent looking is directly associated with more intense and meaningful experiences — but current museum formats rarely support that.

Information is not the bottleneck

Wall labels, audio guides, and video content can deliver facts, but they pull attention away from the act of looking itself. For students and novice visitors, the challenge is not a lack of information. It is a lack of support for knowing what to notice.

Why AR is a candidate, not an obvious answer

Traditional interpretation asks visitors to shift attention between the artwork and an external explanation — a label to the left, a phone screen in hand, an audio track in their ears. Augmented reality could keep interpretation attached to the artwork itself — anchoring context, directing the eye, making discovery spatial. But AR is only justified if it does more than deliver the same content through a more novel interface.

“Design an AR museum experience where movement, close looking, and in-place interpretation are the learning mechanism — not decoration.”

02

State of the Art & Gaps

What existing systems do — and what they miss.

  1. Museum AR overlays

    A 2022 meta-analytic review of 51 museum learning studies found AR and VR produced significant positive effects on achievement and perception. Yet most systems primarily superimpose supplementary materials onto exhibits — stopping short of structured learning interaction. [2]

  2. Engagement ≠ Learning

    One art-museum study comparing AR and book-based guides found AR increased visitors' liking of artworks more, while the book guide produced stronger learning gains. Engagement and learning do not automatically follow each other. [3]

  3. Living-portrait precedent

    Snap's 2026 Jonathan Yeo Spectacles exhibition demonstrated AR-powered portraits that respond to viewers in real time, and the Louvre's Snap collaboration embedded curatorially-grounded AR content directly into gallery visits. Living portraits are viable — but neither is designed around a learning structure. [4][5]

03

The Gap We Target

Structured learning, generative scale, and themed re-playability.

Prior work shows AR can raise engagement, and that living-portrait interaction is technically and culturally viable. What is missing is a system that uses both inside a structured learning experience — one where the interaction teaches visitors how to look, not just what to know. Existing museum AR is also static: the same overlay, the same animation, every visit.

Frames That Speak is an AR museum treasure hunt that guides visitors through a curated set of artworks using spatially anchored clues, artwork-specific overlays, and generatively produced animated portrait interactions. The experience is organized around themed hunts — symbolism, patronage, composition, historical context — so the same gallery yields a meaningfully different learning experience each visit.

The generative layer is what makes it scale. Rather than manually authoring animation assets per artwork, an image-to-video pipeline produces living-portrait moments directly from static painting images. Any artwork becomes a candidate for a portrait interaction without bespoke production work.

“Themed hunts, spatially anchored interpretation, and dynamically produced living portraits turn every gallery visit into a distinct learning experience.”

04

Proposed Approach

Three workstreams, integrated into one experience.

  1. A

    AR Recognition & Anchor Pipeline

    Lead: Arindam Tripathi

    Core AR infrastructure in Unity: image tracking for each artwork, spatial anchoring of overlays to specific visual regions, and the content-delivery pipeline from artwork recognition to in-scene interaction. Built and tested on a mobile AR stack using AR Foundation.

    • Unity
    • AR Foundation
    • ARKit / ARCore

  2. B

    Interaction Engine & Themed Hunt Logic

    Lead: Julian Bauer

    The system that drives the experience: clue progression, theme-based branching logic, prompt triggering, state management across artworks, and the UI flow for treasure-hunt completion. One artwork set supports multiple themed learning paths.

    • C#
    • State machines
    • UI Toolkit

  3. C

    Generative Living Portrait Pipeline

    Lead: Shruti Khetade

    An image-to-video generation workflow that turns static painting images into animated portrait moments, then integrates those outputs into the AR experience. Evaluation of generation quality, consistency, and fit across portrait types and art styles, wired to contextual triggers in the app.

    • Image-to-video models
    • Python
    • Asset pipeline

Workstreams are broad ownership areas, not strict silos. The team collaborated across implementation, integration, and evaluation throughout the semester.

07

Summary & Future Work

Contributions, stretch goals, and a long-term vision.

Contributions

  • An end-to-end AR experience that integrates recognition, anchored interpretation, and generative living portraits in a single flow.
  • A themed-hunt interaction model that turns the same gallery into a different learning path on each visit.
  • A generative pipeline that scales living-portrait moments to new artworks without bespoke animation work.

Future work

  • Pilot study. Compare two themed hunts through the same artwork set; measure focus and recall.
  • Generation quality gating. Automated filters for identity drift, motion artifacts, and tone fit.
  • Glasses-based deployment. Port the experience to platforms such as Snap Spectacles for hands-free, in-gallery use at scale.
  • Curatorial co-authoring. Tools for curators to define new themes and clue chains without engineering support.

Fallbacks honored

  • If generation quality is uneven, a curated baseline of high-quality clips is shipped alongside the live pipeline.
  • If full hunt progression slips, one complete themed interaction (one artwork, one theme) is demonstrated end to end.
